# CHAPTER 1: BELONGING
## Son of Cosmos — Sheldon's POV Outline
## April 3 - April 30, 1989 (28 Days)

---

# THE CENTRAL QUESTION

Sheldon Carter—theoretical physicist, four PhDs, dead at thirty-seven in a particle accelerator cascade failure in Fairbanks, Alaska—wakes in a rice field in rural Japan with a body that isn't his, documents that claim a history he never lived, and a fluency in Japanese he never learned.

**His question for the entire chapter:** *How do I live inside a mystery I cannot solve?*

This is not a question he asks consciously until late in the chapter. For most of it, he's still trying to solve the mystery, cataloging impossibilities, testing hypotheses. But the arc of Chapter 1 is his gradual surrender—not to despair, but to acceptance. By Day 28, he has not solved anything. He has simply stopped requiring solution as a precondition for living.

# PHASE I: ARRIVAL
## Day 1 — April 3, 1989

### The Scientist Confronts the Impossible

**Opening State:** Sheldon's last memory is the cascade failure. The particle accelerator's magnetic containment collapsing. Maria Chen's face. The light that wasn't light.

Then: grass. Warmth. Birdsong. A body that moves when he tells it to, but doesn't match the one he's spent thirty-seven years wearing.

**Internal Conflict:**
- The physicist in him immediately begins cataloging: No scars from the childhood bicycle accident. No calluses on the fingers that spent decades at keyboards. Musculature different—younger, perhaps. Height unchanged. Weight distributed differently.
- He is simultaneously certain he died and certain he is alive. These facts cannot coexist, and yet they do.

**Key Scenes:**

1. **AWAKENING (Field)** — He wakes. Tests his body. Finds the documents in his pocket: Japanese passport, family registry, a letter from an uncle he never had. The name reads "Tsukino Sheldon." The photograph is his face.

2. **THE ROAD** — Walking toward wherever the documents suggest he should go. Every step confirms impossibility. He speaks Japanese without remembering learning it. The countryside is wrong for Alaska but right for something else.

3. **YAMAMOTO ENCOUNTER** — First human contact. An old farmer. Sheldon responds in Japanese automatically, then catches himself doing it. The wrongness compounds.

4. **TSUKINO HOUSE** — His "family home." A building he's never seen, but the key in his pocket fits the lock. Everything inside suggests a life he didn't live. Photographs of people he doesn't know, who share features with his face.

5. **HARUNA'S STORE** — Buying supplies. The shopkeeper knows his "family." Expects him. Has been told he was coming. *By whom?*

6. **THE 320 STEPS** — Climbing to Masaki Shrine. Each step is a physical fact. His body handles the climb better than it should. Another impossibility to file.

7. **FIRST MEETING: KATSUHITO** — An old priest with eyes that see too much. He doesn't ask questions Sheldon expects. Accepts Sheldon's presence as though it were inevitable rather than inexplicable.

8. **TENCHI** — A four-year-old boy who should mean nothing. Takes Sheldon's hand unprompted. Calls him "Sheru-nii" within hours, as though the name had always belonged.

9. **FIRST DINNER** — Sitting at a table with strangers who don't feel like strangers. The priest watches Sheldon with a patience that suggests knowledge he isn't sharing. The child chatters. Sheldon realizes he has nowhere else to go.

10. **FIRST NIGHT** — Lying awake in a room he's been given, cataloging what he knows: He died. He is alive. His body is different. His documents are fabricated. He speaks Japanese. A child has claimed him. An old man has accepted him. None of this makes sense. All of it is true.

**Closing State:** Sheldon does not sleep easily. But he sleeps. Tomorrow he will have to decide what to do with an impossible life.

# PHASE II: ADJUSTMENT
## Days 2-7 — April 4-9, 1989

### Learning to Function in the Inexplicable

**Internal Conflict:**
- The scientist wants data. Every day offers more impossibilities but no explanations.
- The survivor recognizes he has to eat, sleep, exist—regardless of whether he understands why.

**Day 2: First Full Day**

- Morning routine emerges. The shrine has rhythms. Sheldon observes them, begins to participate.
- Katsuhito assigns small tasks. Sweeping. Carrying. Physical labor that requires no context.
- Tenchi goes to kindergarten. Returns in the afternoon. Follows Sheldon everywhere.
- First real conversation with Katsuhito—not about where Sheldon came from, but about the shrine's history. Deflection or genuine disinterest in origins? Sheldon can't tell.
- **Internal note:** Katsuhito never asks the obvious questions. Either he already knows the answers, or he doesn't care about them. Both possibilities are unsettling.

**Day 3: Routine Establishing**

- Sheldon's body continues to surprise him. Stamina he shouldn't have. Balance he didn't possess before.
- He explores the shrine grounds. Finds the path toward the cave. Something about it makes him not want to approach. Not fear, exactly. A wrongness in his gut he can't name.
- Tenchi shows him hiding spots. The child trusts him completely. Sheldon doesn't know what he's done to earn it.
- Dinner conversation reveals: Tenchi's mother is dead. His father works in the city. The child lives here, raised by his grandfather.
- **Internal note:** A motherless child. An absent father. A grandfather priest. And now, Sheldon. What kind of family is this?

**Day 4: COOKING DEBUT** ⭐

- Katsuhito mentions they need to prepare lunch. Sheldon offers to help. 
- Then he's cooking. Not assisting—*cooking*. Knife work he never learned. Techniques he has no memory of practicing. The meal emerges from his hands as though his body knows something his mind doesn't.
- Katsuhito eats without comment. As though this, too, is expected.
- Sheldon eats his own cooking and tastes the impossibility. He is good at this. Professionally good. Skills don't appear from nowhere.
- **Internal note:** First hypothesis: brain damage. Memory loss. He knew these things before and has forgotten learning them. But that doesn't explain the documents, the body, the Japanese. It doesn't explain any of it.

**Day 5: First Village Visit**

- Sheldon descends the 320 steps with Tenchi. First real engagement with the village.
- People recognize him as "the Tsukino nephew." They have expectations. Condolences for an uncle he never met. Questions about his plans for the house.
- The fabricated life has depth. Whoever created it—if someone created it—was thorough.
- Tenchi introduces him to other children. "This is Sheru-nii. He lives with us now."
- **Internal note:** The child has decided. Sheldon lives here. As though it were already true when Sheldon himself isn't sure.

**Day 6: First Weekend**

- Saturday. No kindergarten. Tenchi is present all day.
- Extended bonding. Tenchi shows Sheldon every corner of the shrine he considers important. The sacred tree. The offering box. The spot where cats sometimes come.
- Sheldon realizes he's been here almost a week. He has not tried to leave. Has not contacted anyone from his old life. (Who would he contact? Everyone he knew thinks he's dead. He IS dead.)
- Evening: Katsuhito tells stories. Old myths. Sheldon listens and catches references he shouldn't understand. As though he's heard these stories before, in another context.

**Day 7: First Sunday**

- Shrine visitors arrive. First time Sheldon witnesses Katsuhito in his priestly role.
- The old man transforms—not physically, but in presence. Authority he usually keeps veiled.
- Sheldon helps. Directs visitors, manages logistics. Falls into a role without being assigned one.
- A visitor asks if he's the new shrine assistant. Katsuhito answers before Sheldon can: "He is family."
- **Internal note:** *Family.* The word lands differently than it should. Sheldon has no family. His parents died years ago. No siblings, no children, no wife. He had colleagues, students, research. Now he has a four-year-old boy who calls him "nii-chan" and an old priest who claims him as kin.

**Closing State (End of Week 1):** The hypothesis that this is temporary has quietly died. Sheldon is not waiting to wake up, or to be extracted, or to understand. He is living a day at a time in a place that makes no sense, with people who ask nothing of him except that he be present.

# PHASE III: BELONGING
## Days 8-21 — April 10-23, 1989

### The Unexpected Weight of Connection

**Internal Conflict:**
- Sheldon didn't choose this. Didn't ask for a family, a role, a life in rural Japan.
- But he's starting to want it. And wanting makes the mystery harder, not easier.

**Days 8-10: Transition into Belonging**

- Physical capabilities continue emerging. Gardening on Day 9—his hands know things his mind doesn't. Expansion of the vegetable plot without instruction.
- Rainy day bonding (Day 10). Trapped indoors with Tenchi. They draw together. Sheldon discovers he can sketch. Another skill from nowhere.
- **Internal note:** He's stopped cataloging impossibilities daily. The list would be too long. He's started simply living them.

**Day 11: Teaching Begins**

- Katsuhito begins instructing Sheldon in shrine maintenance. Not labor—*teaching*. As though preparing him for something.
- The old man's patience is bottomless. Explains once. Waits for Sheldon to try. Corrects without criticism.
- Sheldon realizes: Katsuhito is treating him like a student. Not a guest. Not a visitor. Someone who will be here long enough to need these skills.

**Day 12: Second Village Visit**

- Deepening integration. The shopkeeper knows his preferences now. Villagers nod to him by name.
- He overhears someone call him "the Tsukino boy's replacement." Replacement for the uncle who died. Slotted into a space that was waiting.
- **Internal note:** Did someone know this space would be needed? Did someone prepare it?

**Day 13: "SHERU-JI" NICKNAME** ⭐

- Tenchi has been refining. "Sheru-nii" was immediate, instinctive. But now the child tries variations.
- "Sheru-ji" emerges. Not quite uncle, not quite brother. Something between.
- The other children pick it up. Within a day, it's what everyone calls him.
- Sheldon doesn't correct them. The name fits better than "Dr. Carter" ever did.

**Day 14: NOBUYUKI ARRIVES** ⭐

- First meeting with Tenchi's father. A man who works in the city, visits on weekends, loves his son but cannot be present.
- Nobuyuki is warm, slightly chaotic, grateful that someone else is here for Tenchi.
- He doesn't ask where Sheldon came from. Accepts his presence as Katsuhito has. As though Sheldon was always meant to arrive.
- **Internal note:** Three people now treat Sheldon's impossible existence as perfectly natural. Either they all know something he doesn't, or they've all decided not to ask questions. He doesn't know which possibility is more unsettling.

**Day 15: Post-Nobuyuki Reflection**

- Nobuyuki's visit clarifies something. Sheldon isn't replacing Tenchi's mother—no one could. But he's filling a different gap. The daily presence. The continuity the child needs.
- He catches himself thinking of "his" room instead of "the guest room."

**Day 16: CARPENTRY SKILLS EMERGE**

- A fence needs repair. Sheldon picks up tools and his body takes over.
- Joints he never learned to cut. Measurements he calculates without thinking.
- By evening, the fence is better than it was before it broke.
- **Internal note:** The skills keep coming. Each one is a question he can't answer. But the fence is solid. The shrine is maintained. The meals are good. Whatever is happening to him is, at minimum, useful.

**Day 17: Kitten Arrives**

- A stray. Thin, wary, half-feral.
- Tenchi wants to keep it. Katsuhito defers to Sheldon—a decision he probably shouldn't make, but he makes it anyway.
- The kitten stays. Sheldon helps Tenchi name it. (Hikaru? Hinata? The child changes his mind hourly.)
- **Internal note:** He's making decisions for this household. No one has given him authority. He's simply begun exercising it, and no one has stopped him.

**Day 18: FOREST / CAVE / MOTHER** ⭐⭐

- Sheldon moves into the Tsukino house. His own space now. But he's at the shrine every day regardless.
- A walk with Tenchi. The child wants to show him places. They pass the cave entrance.
- Something in Sheldon's gut recoils. Not fear—wrongness. A sensation he can't name but can't ignore. He steers them away without explaining why.
- Then: the conversation about Tenchi's mother. The child mentions her casually. Where she's buried. What she was like. That he barely remembers her face.
- Sheldon listens. Does not offer platitudes. Does not pretend to understand loss he cannot comprehend. Simply listens.
- Tenchi cries a little. Not from grief, exactly—from the exhaustion of holding it. Sheldon's hand on the child's shoulder. No words. Just presence.
- **Internal note:** This is the moment something shifts. Not intellectually—he's known for days that he's attached. But bodily. Cellularly. This child's pain registers as his own. The scientist in him notes: this is how families work. The man in him stops analyzing and simply holds on.

**Days 19-21: Birthday Preparations**

- Tenchi turns five in three days. Four days. Two days.
- Sheldon finds himself planning. Coordinating with Katsuhito. Talking to Nobuyuki by phone about what the child wants.
- He's cooking a birthday meal. Baking a cake (another skill from nowhere). Carving a small wooden figure (the carpentry again).
- **Internal note:** He's invested. Not because he chose to be, but because the alternative—not caring—has become impossible.

**Closing State (End of Week 3):** Sheldon's hypothesis has shifted. He no longer asks "why am I here?" He asks "what do I do now that I'm here?" The mystery remains. But it's no longer the central question.

# PHASE IV: HOME
## Days 22-28 — April 24-30, 1989

### Certainty Without Understanding

**Internal Conflict:**
- Sheldon accepts that he may never understand how he got here.
- The harder question: Does it matter?

**Day 22: TENCHI'S 5TH BIRTHDAY** ⭐⭐⭐

- The chapter's first major harvest. Everything built over three weeks flowers at once.
- Morning preparations. The shrine decorated. Nobuyuki arrives the night before.
- Tenchi wakes to a household ready to celebrate him. He doesn't understand why this birthday feels different—only that it does.
- **Birthday Dinner** — Everyone Sheldon has met in three weeks, gathered. Village friends. The priest's few remaining contacts. Nobuyuki, relaxed for once. And Sheldon, who three weeks ago didn't know any of these people existed.
- **Presents Opening** — Sheldon gives Tenchi the carved figure. It's small—a cat, roughly shaped, clearly made by someone learning. Tenchi holds it like treasure.
- **Internal note:** The child's joy is uncomplicated. Pure. Sheldon realizes he's never given someone a gift that mattered this much. In his old life, he gave books, equipment, charitable donations. Never something his hands made for a specific person.

**Days 23-25: Post-Birthday Settling**

- The heightened energy dissipates. Normal routine reasserts.
- Sheldon notices he's stopped thinking of Alaska as "home." It was where he worked. Where he died. But it was never this.
- **Homework Help** (Day 24) — Tenchi brings home simple kindergarten work. Sheldon helps. The child's focus is terrible; Sheldon's patience is infinite. Another surprise.
- **Philosophical Talk** (Day 25) — Rainy day. Katsuhito and Sheldon on the engawa. The old man doesn't ask questions directly, but his observations suggest he knows more than he's saying.
  - Katsuhito: "Some things arrive when they're needed."
  - Sheldon: "And if you don't know why they're needed?"
  - Katsuhito: "Then you wait. Understanding isn't always required."

**Days 26-28: Golden Week Approach**

- Japan's national holiday period begins. The shrine will be busy.
- Sheldon prepares without being asked. He knows what's needed now.
- **Shōwa Day** (Day 27) — Visitors in larger numbers. Sheldon functions as unofficial assistant. He's good at it. The role fits.
- **Internal note:** He's useful here. Not because of his four PhDs or his research or anything he built in thirty-seven years of his old life. Useful because he shows up, does work, cares about a child, respects an old man.

**Day 27: Quiet Sunday**

- Eve of culmination. The household feels different—settled in a way it wasn't before Sheldon arrived.
- He walks the grounds alone. Passes the cave entrance. The wrongness is still there, but fainter. Or he's learned to live beside it.
- **Internal note:** Something is in that cave. He doesn't know what. Doesn't need to know. His life exists outside it.

**Day 28: "HOME" STATEMENT** ⭐⭐⭐

- The chapter's climax. Not a dramatic event—a quiet recognition.
- Morning: routine. Afternoon: chores. Evening: dinner with Tenchi and Katsuhito.
- After dinner, on the engawa. Tenchi falls asleep against Sheldon's side. The old man drinks tea. The sun sets.
- Sheldon realizes he's stopped waiting. Not waiting to understand. Not waiting to leave. Not waiting for the mystery to resolve.
- He lives here. This is where he lives.
- **The Line (internal):** He doesn't say it aloud. But it crystallizes: *This is home. I don't know how. I don't know why. But this is home.*

# THREADS OPEN AT CHAPTER END

**Resolved:**
- SHRINE INTEGRATION — Complete. Sheldon has a role.
- FOUND FAMILY — Harvesting. The bonds are real.

**Active (Continues to Chapter 2):**
- IDENTITY MYSTERY — Sheldon still doesn't know how he got here, who made his documents, why his body is different, where the skills come from. The mystery is unresolved. He has simply stopped requiring resolution.
- 1989 JAPAN LIFE — He's adapting. But there will be more adjustments ahead.
- COMMUNITY — Growing. The village knows him, but doesn't truly know him. This will deepen.

# THE READER'S IRONY

Throughout Chapter 1, the reader knows what Sheldon doesn't:
- Sailor Cosmos sent him here
- Katsuhito is a 700-year-old Juraian prince hiding in plain sight
- The cave contains Ryoko, sealed for 700 years
- Tenchi will one day release her
- Everything that happens in canonical Tenchi Muyo begins in twelve years

Sheldon knows none of this. His experience is purely ground-level: an impossible situation, an old priest, a child, a shrine, a village. No cosmic significance. No awareness of what's coming.

This is the dramatic irony that will sustain the next twelve years of narrative: Sheldon building a life in a place whose true nature he cannot perceive, loving people whose secrets he doesn't know, preparing for events he cannot imagine.
