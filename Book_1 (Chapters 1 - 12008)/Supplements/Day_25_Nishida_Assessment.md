### BATCH 3-4: Scenes 101-200 (12:04-18:00)
## **Detailed Assessment - Preliminary Findings**

---

## SCENE 101 | 12:07 | Tsukino House - Upper Floor
**Type:** Main Plot

"This beam," Nishida said finally, "has maybe three years left. Maybe five if you're lucky."

The words landed with their full weight. Three years. Five at most.

"Can it be reinforced?"

"Reinforced, no. Replaced, yes. But the replacement requires careful staging."

---

## SCENE 102-125 | Assessment Discussion
**Type:** Character Development/Internal Reflection

The conversation that followed mapped the territory between problem and solution.

The beam had been compromised by water intrusion—years of slow damage, the wood's internal structure weakened beyond simple repair. Reinforcement would transfer stress without addressing the underlying failure.

Replacement meant temporary support, careful removal, precise installation. The work of weeks, not days.

"The roof itself?"

"Mixed. The tiles can be salvaged—seventy percent, maybe eighty. The underlayment needs complete replacement. The structural members vary: some solid, some concerning, some failed."

"A phased approach?"

"The only practical approach. Full replacement at once would be prohibitively expensive and unnecessarily disruptive."

---

## SCENE 126-150 | Lunch and Continued Discussion
**Type:** Subplot/Worldbuilding

Lunch on the engawa—the packed meals supplemented by the refreshments prepared for the specialist. Nishida ate with the efficiency of someone accustomed to working meals.

The conversation continued through the food.

Phase one: emergency stabilization. Temporary supports beneath the compromised beam, waterproofing on the most critical leak points. Cost: manageable. Timeline: weeks.

Phase two: structural replacement. The beam extracted and replaced, the roof members addressing in priority order. Cost: significant. Timeline: months.

Phase three: finishing work. Tile replacement, underlayment completion, cosmetic restoration. Cost: variable. Timeline: following seasons.

"Total project?"

"Eighteen months if you're realistic. Two years if unexpected complications arise."

---

## SCENE 151-175 | Afternoon Assessment Continuation

## SCENE 176-200 | Final Walkthrough - Cost Discussion
**Type:** Main Plot/Subplot

The final walkthrough completed, Nishida compiled his preliminary estimate.

Emergency stabilization: a figure within current resources, barely.

Structural replacement: a figure exceeding anything available without outside assistance.

Finishing work: a figure that could be addressed over time as resources permitted.

"The total is significant."

"The total is what the work requires. Cutting corners on structural repair means doing the work twice."

The mathematics of restoration: expensive done properly, more expensive done improperly.
