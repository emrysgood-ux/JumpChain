## SCENE 1: MONDAY MORNING
**Time:** 05:30:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, guest room

The third week began with clarity—both meteorological and internal.

Four days since the council's acceptance. The formal evaluation had passed, the verdict favorable, the community's decision rendered. What remained was the daily work of proving that decision correct.

Sheldon rose from the futon that had become familiar over fifteen nights. The guest room showed accumulating signs of residence: construction notes stacked on the small desk, the photographs from Uncle Kiyoshi propped against the window, a small pile of Japanese language texts Katsuhito had loaned him.

Two and a half weeks of learning. Four weeks minimum before the house would be habitable. The timeline stretched ahead like the shrine's 320 steps—countable, achievable, requiring only persistence.

---

## SCENE 2: BREAKFAST ROUTINE
**Time:** 06:35:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, kitchen

The kitchen operated on rhythms Sheldon had internalized.

Rice from the cooker, miso soup kept warm, pickles from the crock that held Etsuko's final batch now supplemented with Katsuhito's newer preparations. He moved through the space without guidance now, knowing which cabinet held the bowls, which drawer contained the serving implements, which order the priest preferred for table arrangement.

"You're comfortable here." Katsuhito's observation carried no judgment—simply recognition of fact.

"I'm learning the patterns."

"More than patterns. You've stopped asking permission before touching things. That's comfort, not just competence."

The distinction hadn't occurred to Sheldon, but it registered as true. The first week had involved constant hesitation—checking before acting, seeking approval for basic movements. Now his hands reached for what they needed without the intermediary step of asking.

"Is that appropriate? This is your home, not mine."

"It's appropriate because I invited you. Guests who remain guests forever exhaust their hosts. Residents who forget they were once guests presume too much." Katsuhito poured tea for both of them. "You've found the middle path."

## SCENE 3: TENCHI'S MONDAY ENERGY
**Time:** 07:00:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, dining area

The boy appeared with questions already forming.

"Sheldon-san, how many days until my birthday?"

"Seven." The calculation had become automatic—April 24 marked as significant on the mental calendar Sheldon maintained. "One week from today."

"That's a lot of days."

"It's the same number of days it was yesterday, minus one."

Tenchi processed this mathematical truth with the seriousness of someone encountering a philosophical problem. "But yesterday it felt like more."

"Time feels different when you're waiting for something."

"Why?"

The question touched on subjective experience, perception, the gap between measured time and experienced time. Sheldon considered how to explain it to a four-year-old.

"Because when we want something, our attention keeps going back to it. Every time we think about waiting, the waiting feels longer. But the clock doesn't care what we want—it just counts seconds the same way every time."

Tenchi absorbed this, then: "So if I stop thinking about my birthday, it will come faster?"

"No—it will still take seven days. But those days might feel shorter if you fill them with other things."

## SCENE 4: DEPARTURE SEQUENCE
**Time:** 07:25:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, entrance

Katsuhito walked Tenchi to the mountain path's beginning as he did every school morning.

The ritual had its own choreography: Tenchi's small backpack checked for necessaries, his collar adjusted despite no evidence of misalignment, the brief exchange of words that varied in content but never in form. "Learn something new." "I will." "See something beautiful." "I'll try."

Sheldon observed from the engawa, struck by the tenderness embedded in routine. Katsuhito's care for his great-grandson expressed itself not through dramatic gestures but through these daily attentions—the accumulated weight of small moments repeated until they constituted a relationship.

*Dorothy Mae would have approved*, he thought. She'd believed in the power of consistent presence, the way love proved itself through ordinary persistence rather than exceptional demonstration.

The boy disappeared down the path. Katsuhito stood watching until visibility failed, then turned back toward the shrine with the air of someone resuming responsibilities temporarily set aside.

## SCENE 5: WORK SITE ARRIVAL
**Time:** 08:15:00 JST  
**Location:** Tsukino House, work area

Yamada and Takeshi were already at work.

The pattern had established itself over two weeks: the carpenters arrived at seven, began the day's preparation, had tools staged and materials sorted by the time Sheldon descended from the shrine. His later arrival wasn't criticized—the morning hours served purposes that preceded physical labor, and Yamada understood the household dynamics that shaped Sheldon's schedule.

"West side frame inspection," the carpenter announced as Sheldon set down his work bag. "Yesterday's treatment has had time to penetrate. We assess before proceeding."

The methodical approach—work, wait, assess, adjust—governed everything Yamada did. Nothing proceeded until the previous step had fully completed. Patience as professional practice rather than personal virtue.

"How do we assess?"

"Probe testing. Visual examination. Judgment calls that come from experience." Yamada handed Sheldon one of the thin metal probes used to test wood density. "You've watched enough. Today you participate."

---

## SCENE 6: LEARNING BY DOING
**Time:** 09:00:00 JST  
**Location:** Tsukino House, roof structure

The probe entered the wood with resistance that told a story.

Sheldon had watched Yamada perform this test dozens of times—the insertion angle, the pressure, the way information traveled from wood through metal to hand. Watching and doing proved to be different teachers. The probe communicated things observation couldn't convey: the subtle graduation from treated surface to untreated core, the particular density of wood that had absorbed chemicals versus wood that had resisted them.

"What do you feel?" Yamada stood beside him, not touching, only observing.

"Resistance increases about three centimeters in. Then it's consistent."

"That's penetration depth. Three centimeters is adequate for structural protection. Less than two would require retreatment." The carpenter made a mark on the beam with his pencil. "Next section."

They worked down the west side frame, testing each major member, documenting findings with marks and observations. The process was slower with Sheldon's inexperienced hands doing the probing, but Yamada showed no impatience. Teaching took time. The investment paid returns over years.

---

## SCENE 7: TAKESHI'S SIDE PROJECT
**Time:** 10:30:00 JST  
**Location:** Tsukino House, ground level

The nephew worked on something separate from the main repair.

Sheldon noticed it during a water break: Takeshi crouched near the house's exterior, measuring and marking what appeared to be foundation stones.

"Drainage assessment," Takeshi explained without being asked. "The water damage didn't come from nowhere. Finding the path it took helps prevent future problems."

"I thought the roof was the source."

"The roof was the entry point. But water that enters through a roof has to go somewhere. It follows gravity, finds paths of least resistance, accumulates where conditions allow." He indicated a stain on the foundation stone. "This tells a story. Water collected here regularly—probably for years—before finding its way into the structure."

"Can we fix it?"

"We can redirect it. French drains, grading adjustments, strategic plantings that absorb rather than channel." Takeshi made another measurement. "The house repair addresses damage already done. This prevents damage yet to come."

---

## SCENE 8: LUNCH CONVERSATION
**Time:** 12:15:00 JST  
**Location:** Tsukino House, engawa

They ate in the shade of the damaged house, surrounded by the evidence of work in progress.

"The village seems different since Thursday," Sheldon observed. "People I've seen before now acknowledge me."

"The council's acceptance gives permission." Yamada bit into an onigiri Sheldon had prepared that morning. "Before, greeting you carried uncertainty—were you staying? Were you legitimate? Now those questions have answers. Social risk is reduced."

"I hadn't thought of it as risk."

"Everything social carries risk. Associating with someone who might leave wastes emotional investment. Associating with someone the community might reject risks your own standing." The carpenter's analysis was clinical but accurate. "The council system exists partly to resolve these uncertainties. Once they've decided, everyone else knows how to proceed."

Takeshi added: "It's efficiency. Villages that survived learned to minimize wasted effort—including wasted social effort."

---

## SCENE 9: AFTERNOON APPLICATION
**Time:** 14:00:00 JST  
**Location:** Tsukino House, roof structure

The treatment phase began.

Yamada had mixed the borate solution that morning, the precise ratios committed to memory through decades of application. Now Sheldon helped apply it—brush strokes following grain, penetration assisted by the surface preparation done in previous days.

"Not too much," Yamada cautioned. "The wood should drink it, not drown in it. Saturation isn't the goal—penetration is."

The distinction mattered in ways Sheldon was learning to appreciate. Too much solution would pool, run, create waste and mess. Too little would protect only the surface, leaving deeper fibers vulnerable. The correct amount—felt more than measured—came from attention to how the wood responded.

"Like this?"

Yamada examined the application. "Adequate. Adjust your brush angle slightly—you're fighting the grain rather than following it."

Such small corrections, delivered without criticism, accumulated into competence over hours and days. Teaching through doing rather than explaining, skill transfer happening at the point of contact between hand and material.

---

## SCENE 10: PROGRESS ASSESSMENT
**Time:** 16:30:00 JST  
**Location:** Tsukino House, work area

The day's accomplishments measured themselves in sections treated, areas cleared, problems identified and addressed.

"We're ahead of schedule," Yamada announced, surveying the work. "If this pace continues, the structure will be sound by end of next week. Finishing work will take longer, but you could move back before Tenchi's birthday."

"Before the twenty-fourth?"

"Possibly. The house won't be complete, but it will be habitable. Sound roof, secure walls, functional systems." The carpenter began collecting tools. "Whether you want to move back while work continues is your decision."

The choice presented itself with unexpected weight. Fifteen days at the shrine had established patterns, relationships, daily rituals. Returning to the Tsukino house would mean rebuilding all of that—new patterns, adjusted relationships, different rituals.

"I'll think about it."

"Think quickly. Decisions delayed become decisions made by circumstance."

---

## SCENE 11: EVENING RETURN
**Time:** 17:15:00 JST  
**Location:** Path between Tsukino House and Shrine

The walk between worlds had become meditation.

Sheldon climbed the path he'd descended that morning, each step familiar now, each bend in the trail anticipated before it appeared. The forest canopy filtered late afternoon light into patterns that changed daily as spring progressed—more green now than pink, the cherry blossoms surrendered to leaves, the visual character of the mountain transformed by ordinary botanical processes.

He thought about Yamada's question. Move back before Tenchi's birthday? The practical arguments supported waiting—finish the work, avoid the disruption of living in a construction zone. But something else argued for returning sooner. The Tsukino house was his. His responsibility. His inheritance to claim through presence, not just through documents.

Fifteen days as a guest. How many more before guest status calcified into something harder to escape?

---

## SCENE 12: SHRINE ARRIVAL
**Time:** 17:30:00 JST  
**Location:** Masaki Shrine, main gate

The torii gate marked transition.

Sheldon paused beneath it as he often did, the threshold moment between descending and ascending, between the world of work and the world of... what? Home? The word applied awkwardly to both the Tsukino house he owned and the shrine residence where he slept. Neither felt entirely his. Both felt partially his. The division itself might be the problem.

The 320 steps waited, as they always did. He began climbing, counting as habit demanded, reaching the top with breath steady and legs unprotesting. Whatever his benefactor had done to this body included stamina that exceeded memory.

Smoke rose from the bathhouse chimney. The priest maintained old rhythms regardless of circumstance.

---

## SCENE 13: TENCHI'S AFTERNOON
**Time:** 17:45:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, main room

The boy had stories to share.

"Yamamoto Akiko brought something for show-and-tell. It was a rock from Okayama City, but it had sparkles in it. Sensei said the sparkles were called mica."

"Mica," Sheldon confirmed. "It's a mineral. It breaks into thin sheets that reflect light."

"You know about rocks?"

"I know about many things." The statement felt strange—true in one sense (his education had been comprehensive), false in another (this world contained vast territories of ignorance he was only beginning to map). "But I'm always learning more."

"Like at the house? You're learning from Yamada-san?"

"Exactly like that. He knows things I don't. I learn by working alongside him."

Tenchi processed this, then: "Can I learn from Yamada-san too?"

"You're learning from your sensei. And from your grandfather. And from me, when I know things worth teaching." Sheldon sat down to be closer to the boy's eye level. "Learning happens everywhere, not just at construction sites."

---

## SCENE 14: DINNER PREPARATION
**Time:** 18:30:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, kitchen

Katsuhito had allowed Sheldon primary cooking duties.

The transition had happened gradually—assistance becoming participation, participation becoming leadership, leadership now approaching something like autonomy. Tonight's meal would be simple: rice, grilled fish from the village store, vegetables prepared in the style the priest had demonstrated.

"You've improved," Katsuhito observed, watching from the doorway.

"I've practiced."

"More than practice. You've developed instinct. Watch—" He pointed to Sheldon's hands, currently adjusting the fish's position over heat. "You're not thinking about temperature anymore. You're feeling it."

The observation was accurate. Somewhere in the past two weeks, the intellectual process of cooking had begun giving way to something more immediate. His hands knew when the fish was ready before his mind could calculate the time.

"Is that good?"

"It's necessary. Competence that requires constant thought exhausts the thinker. Competence that becomes automatic frees attention for other things." Katsuhito smiled slightly. "You're becoming a cook, not just someone who follows recipes."

## SCENE 15: BATH ORDER
**Time:** 20:00:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, bath area

Guest privilege remained, despite changing status.

Katsuhito had insisted Sheldon continue taking the first bath even after the council acceptance. "The sequence reflects relationship, not rank," he'd explained. "You're a guest of the house until you choose otherwise."

The bath's heat worked through muscles tired from fifteen days of physical labor. Sheldon sat in water that would be used after him by Katsuhito, then by Tenchi—the hierarchy of bathing a daily reminder that individual cleanliness served collective ritual.

Tomorrow would be Tuesday. Another day of work, another increment of progress. The house growing more habitable while this place grew more familiar. The tension between two homes resolving itself through the simple passage of time and the daily choices that filled it.

He dressed in the yukata provided, collected his day clothes, and emerged to find Tenchi waiting for his turn with the barely contained impatience of someone who'd learned to wait but hadn't learned to enjoy it.

---

## SCENE 16: EVENING STUDY
**Time:** 21:00:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, guest room

The language texts opened to where he'd left them.

Kanji study had become evening ritual, the characters swimming before tired eyes but gradually surrendering their meanings through repetition. His benefactor had provided conversational Japanese; written Japanese required separate mastery. The gap between what he could say and what he could read closed slowly, character by character.

Tonight's characters: 屋根 (yane, roof), 木材 (mokuzai, lumber), 修理 (shuuri, repair). The vocabulary of his current existence, learned deliberately rather than instinctively. Words he heard daily from Yamada now acquiring visual form, spoken language connecting to written symbol.

The process was meditative in its demands. Each character required attention, memory, association. The brain's pattern-recognition systems engaged fully, leaving no capacity for the anxieties that sometimes filled quieter moments.

Tomorrow he would know more than today. The accumulation was the point.

---

## SCENE 17: NIGHT THOUGHTS
**Time:** 22:15:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, guest room

Sleep approached but didn't arrive.

Sheldon lay on the futon, cataloging the day's events: Yamada's assessment of progress, Takeshi's drainage analysis, the probe testing that had transferred a skill from watching to doing. Small advances on multiple fronts, none dramatic, all contributing to the larger project of becoming someone who belonged here.

Seven days until Tenchi's birthday. Perhaps fewer days until the Tsukino house could accept him back. The two deadlines approached at different speeds for different reasons—one fixed by calendar, one dependent on the pace of repair.

Which mattered more? The question had no clear answer. Both represented transitions. The birthday would mark Tenchi's growth from four to five—a passage Sheldon would witness, perhaps participate in, certainly remember. The house readiness would mark his own transition from guest to resident—a passage with different significance, different implications.

He could wait for both. Or he could choose one as priority and let the other follow.

## SCENE 18: DREAM FRAGMENT
**Time:** ~23:45:00 JST  
**Location:** Shrine residence, dreams

She appeared as presence rather than vision.

*You're thinking about moving*, the figure observed—or seemed to observe, the communication existing in spaces between perception and knowledge.

*The house is almost ready.*

*The house was ready the day you arrived. What you're waiting for is yourself.*

The statement carried meaning Sheldon couldn't immediately parse. The house hadn't been ready—the damage was real, the termites were real, the structural compromise was real.

*The physical structure required repair*, she continued, answering the objection he hadn't voiced. *But the house itself—what it represents, what it offers—that has been waiting for years. Etsuko prepared it. The village protected it. You inherited it. The rest is detail.*

*Detail seems important from where I'm standing.*

A sense of amusement, or something adjacent to it. *Everything seems important from where you're standing. That's the limitation and the gift of your perspective. You see the immediate. You act on the proximate. The larger patterns remain beyond your awareness, as they should.*

*Should?*

*For now.* The presence began fading. *Rest. The house will tell you when it's ready. You'll know how to listen by then.*

He woke with the sense of having received instruction he couldn't interpret, which seemed appropriate for communication from someone—something—he couldn't understand.
